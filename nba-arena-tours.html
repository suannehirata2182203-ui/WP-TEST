Inside NBA Arenas

A fan's honest review of 28 arenas and counting

Home / NBA Arena Tours

Photo by TJ Dragotta on Unsplash

Author: Marcus Reynolds Last Updated: September 15, 2025 Reading Time: 15 minutes

I set a goal in 2018: visit every NBA arena in America. As of last month, I've been to 28 of 30. Only Salt Lake City and Portland remain on my list, and I've got tickets for both before the All-Star break.

What I've discovered is that NBA arenas are wildly inconsistent. Some are architectural marvels that enhance every moment of the game. Others feel like they were designed by someone who's never actually watched basketball. Here's my unfiltered take.

The Absolute Best Arenas in the League

Madison Square Garden, New York

I know, I know, it's the obvious choice. But there's a reason every player wants to have a big game at MSG. The arena is intimate in a way that modern buildings can't replicate. When I saw the Knicks play the Lakers there in 2022, the energy was unlike anything I'd experienced.

The location in midtown Manhattan adds to the experience. You can grab dinner at Katz's Deli beforehand, walk through Penn Station, and suddenly you're in basketball history. Tickets are expensive, but this is one arena where I genuinely think it's worth the premium.

"Every seat at MSG feels like you're part of the game, not just watching it. The building amplifies the crowd noise in a way that modern acoustics engineers can't quite figure out."

Photo by Markus Spiske on Unsplash

Chase Center, San Francisco

The Warriors' new home is what happens when unlimited money meets actual thoughtful design. I visited in March 2024 for a game against the Celtics, and the attention to detail was staggering.

What impressed me most wasn't the technology, though the video boards are pristine. It's the sight lines. Every seat, even in the upper deck corners, gives you a complete view of the court. That sounds basic, but you'd be surprised how many arenas get this wrong.

The neighborhood around the arena is still developing, but there are already some solid food options. Grab tacos at Underdogs Too before the game, and you're set.

Crypto.com Arena, Los Angeles

The arena formerly known as Staples Center has seen better days in terms of the Lakers' on-court product, but the building itself remains top-tier. It hosts both the Lakers and Clippers, plus concerts and awards shows, so the infrastructure is built to handle anything.

Pro tip: walk around the exterior before you go in. The statues of Lakers legends, including Magic, Kareem, and Shaq, are genuinely moving tributes. The Chick Hearn statue gets me every time.

The Arenas That Surprised Me

Fiserv Forum, Milwaukee

I had low expectations for Milwaukee. I was completely wrong. Fiserv Forum opened in 2018, and it's one of the best-designed arenas in the league. The Bucks have created a genuine entertainment district around the building, with bars and restaurants that stay packed on game nights.

The arena itself has excellent sight lines, reasonable concession prices by NBA standards, and a crowd that actually pays attention to the game. Wisconsin basketball fans are intense in the best way.

Smoothie King Center, New Orleans

The name is ridiculous, I'll grant you that. But the arena experience is legitimately great. New Orleans fans are passionate, the building has good bones, and the location puts you right in the middle of the city's action.

I timed my visit to coincide with a Saints weekend in 2023, which meant the whole city was in party mode. Watching the Pelicans on a Friday night, then hitting Bourbon Street afterward, is a combination I'd recommend to anyone.

The Arenas That Need Work

I'm not trying to be negative, but some arenas genuinely need renovation or replacement. Here's my honest assessment:

Capital One Arena, Washington D.C.

It pains me to say this because D.C. is a great sports town, but Capital One Arena is showing its age. The concourses are cramped, the concession options are mediocre, and the building lacks the personality you'd expect from a venue in the nation's capital.

The Wizards are discussing renovations, and I hope they happen. The bones of the building are fine, but it needs a refresh to compete with newer arenas.

Barclays Center, Brooklyn

This one might be controversial. Barclays looks great from the outside, and it's in a prime Brooklyn location. But the actual basketball experience is disappointing. The sight lines are awkward for NBA games because the building was designed primarily for hockey. You can literally feel the angle being wrong.

The Nets have a dedicated fanbase that deserves better. Hopefully, the reported Hamptons arena project gives them a true home.

Practical Arena Tips

Getting the Best Seats

NBA ticket markets are more volatile than NFL or MLB, which can work in your favor:

  • For big matchups: Buy at least a week in advance. Lakers, Warriors, and Celtics games always command premiums.
  • For regular games: Check prices 2-3 hours before tipoff. Prices often drop significantly on weeknight games.
  • Best value seats: Corners of the lower bowl, about 15-20 rows up. You're close to the action without paying midcourt prices.

What to Bring

Most NBA arenas have strict bag policies now. Here's what I've learned:

  • Clear bags under 12x12 inches are universally accepted
  • Phone chargers are essential because Uber surge pricing after games is real
  • Layers, since arena temperatures vary wildly between buildings

For official arena policies and ticket information, visit the NBA Tickets page.

My Arena Bucket List

If you're planning to hit multiple arenas, here's the trip I'd recommend for a first-timer:

  1. New York: MSG is mandatory. Catch a Knicks game, soak in the history.
  2. Boston: TD Garden is small but mighty. The Celtics tradition is palpable.
  3. Chicago: United Center has the Jordan statue and incredible fan energy.
  4. San Francisco: Chase Center is the future of arena design.

That four-city trip hits different eras of NBA architecture and culture. You'll see everything from 1960s intimacy to 2020s technology.

Final Thoughts

NBA arenas have evolved dramatically over the past two decades. The league has pushed teams to invest in fan experience, and most have responded. But there's still a gap between the best and worst venues that's larger than you might expect.

My advice: do your research before buying expensive tickets. A great seat in an average arena might be less enjoyable than a decent seat in an exceptional one. The building matters more than casual fans realize.
